2017

2018
Draft BESS Policy — MoP issues guidelines for procurement; pilot projects in Andaman & Nicobar (1 MWh), Lakshadweep (2 MWh).

SECI First Tender (10 MW) — India's first utility BESS at NTPC Simhadri, AP (100 MW / 130 MWh). Tariff discovered: ₹12.1/kWh.
2021
ACC PLI Scheme — ₹18,100 Cr PLI; 7 winners awarded total 50 GWh. Targets cells + modules by 2025–26.
2022
CERC Tariff Framework — Draft regulations for standalone BESS tariff. VGF framework to bridge viability gap to ₹6.50/kWh.
2023
VGF Phase 1 + GWh Tenders — SECI issues India's first 1 GWh BESS tender. VGF Phase 1 notified (₹3,760 Cr initial tranche; scheme later expanded to 13.2 GWh total). NEP 2023 sets 208 GWh energy storage target by FY30. Ancillary services market opened.
2024
Multiple GWh Tenders — SECI, NTPC, state DISCOMs issue 3.3+ GWh. CERC allows BESS in Primary/Secondary Response. VGF Phase 1 (4 GWh) awards begin.
2025
Active: 6 Open Tenders (~6 GWh) — First PLI-backed LFP cells expected from domestic fabs. MSEDCL, TPDDL bids open. NTPC 500 MWh construction begins.
2026
Mandatory Co-location — Proposed: all new solar >200 MW must include BESS. First domestic cells enter market (Ola, Reliance).
2027
10 GWh Milestone — NESM intermediate target. Secondary battery recycling policy expected. BESS eligible for REC trading proposed.
2030
NEP 2023 Target: 208 GWh energy / 41.65 GW power — Embedded in 500 GW RE programme. VGF Phase 1+2: ₹9,160 Cr for 43.2 GWh. India projected to reach 346 GWh cumulative by 2033 (IEEFA 2026). Estimated ₹3.5 lakh crore cumulative investment to 2030.

Step 1 — Convert Inputs to Common Units
All user inputs are in mixed units. The first step is conversion to INR and kWh before any formula is applied.
Capacity: cap_kwh = cap (MWh) × 1,000 [kWh] Capex (INR): capex_inr = cap_kwh × cpx ($/kWh) × fx (₹/$) = cap × 1,000 × cpx × fx [₹] Default example (100 MWh, $128/kWh, ₹84/$): capex_inr = 100 × 1,000 × 128 × 84 = ₹107.52 Cr
VGF adjustment: If a VGF (Viability Gap Funding) subsidy is set in ₹/kWh, it is treated as a grant received at commissioning (Year 0) and reduces the effective capex:
vgf_grant = vgf (₹/kWh) × cap_kwh capex_net = max(0, capex_inr − vgf_grant)
Step 2 — Annual Energy Flows
Two energy quantities matter: energy dispatched to the grid (what earns revenue) and energy drawn from the grid to charge (what costs money). Round-trip efficiency (RTE) and depth-of-discharge (DoD) link them.
Dispatched energy (annual): e_dispatch = cap_kwh × cycles × (DoD / 100) [kWh/yr] Charging energy (annual): e_input = e_dispatch / (RTE / 100) [kWh/yr] Default example (100 MWh, 300 cycles, 90% DoD, 87% RTE): e_dispatch = 100,000 × 300 × 0.90 = 27,000,000 kWh/yr = 27 GWh/yr e_input = 27,000,000 / 0.87 = 31,034,483 kWh/yr ≈ 31 GWh/yr
⚠ RTE does NOT appear in the LCOE denominator directly. LCOE is defined over dispatched energy. RTE affects the charging cost (you pay for more input than you dispatch), not the dispatched quantity itself.
Step 3 — LCOE (Levelised Cost of Energy Storage)
LCOE answers: "what tariff must this project earn per kWh dispatched to exactly recover all costs (capex + O&M) at the chosen discount rate, ignoring revenue?"
Capital Recovery Factor: \[ CRF = \dfrac{r\,(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n-1} \] where r = discount rate, n = lifetime (years) Annual O&M: om_annual = capex_inr × (om_pct / 100) LCOE: \[ LCOE = \dfrac{capex_{INR} \cdot CRF + om_{annual}}{E_{dispatch}} \quad [\text{₹/kWh}] \] Default example (10% DR, 15yr life, 1.5% O&M): CRF = 0.10 × (1.10)^15 / ((1.10)^15 − 1) = 0.10 × 4.177 / 3.177 = 0.1315 om_annual = 107.52 Cr × 0.015 = ₹1.61 Cr LCOE = (107.52 Cr × 0.1315 + 1.61 Cr) / 27,000,000 = (14.14 + 1.61) Cr / 27,000,000 kWh = 15.75 Cr / 27,000,000 kWh = ₹5.83/kWh
⚠ LCOE is a cost metric, not a profitability metric. A project is viable if the market tariff exceeds LCOE. The "Break-even Tariff" (Step 6) is a revenue-side LCOE that includes charging costs.
Step 4 — Annual Revenue & Cost Structure
Annual net cash flow (Years 1 to N) is the difference between storage revenue, charging cost, and O&M.
Revenue: rev_annual = e_dispatch × tariff (₹/kWh) Charging: chg_annual = e_input × chg_cost (₹/kWh) O&M: om_annual = capex_inr × (om_pct / 100) Net annual CF = rev_annual − chg_annual − om_annual Default example (₹7.5 tariff, ₹3.5 charging): rev_annual = 27,000,000 × 7.5 = ₹20.25 Cr chg_annual = 31,034,483 × 3.5 = ₹10.86 Cr om_annual = ₹1.61 Cr Net annual = 20.25 − 10.86 − 1.61 = ₹7.78 Cr/yr Cash flow stream: [−107.52, +7.78, +7.78, ... (×15 years)]
Step 5 — NPV (Net Present Value)
NPV sums all cash flows discounted to today's money. A positive NPV means the project creates value above the hurdle rate (discount rate).
\[ NPV = \sum_{t=0}^{N} \dfrac{CF_t}{(1+r)^t} \] = CF_0 + CF_1/(1+r) + CF_2/(1+r)² + ... + CF_N/(1+r)^N CF_0 = −capex_net (capital outlay at Year 0) CF_t = net_annual (for t = 1, 2, ..., N) Default: NPV = −107.52 + 7.78×Σ[1/(1.1)^t, t=1..15] = −107.52 + 7.78 × 7.606 [annuity factor] = −107.52 + 59.19 = −₹48.3 Cr (negative at default tariff) Raise tariff to ₹9/kWh: Net annual ≈ ₹14.1 Cr → NPV ≈ +₹0.0 Cr (breakeven)
Step 6 — IRR (Internal Rate of Return)
IRR is the discount rate at which NPV = 0. It answers: "what return does this project earn on invested capital?" The code uses a bisection search (200 iterations between 0% and 500%) converging to ±0.001% accuracy.
Find r* such that: \[ \sum_{t=0}^{N} \dfrac{CF_t}{(1+r^*)^t} = 0 \] Algorithm: lo=0, hi=5 (500%) for 200 iterations: mid = (lo + hi) / 2 if NPV(mid) > 0: lo = mid else: hi = mid IRR ≈ (lo + hi) / 2 Shown in red if IRR < discount rate (project underperforms hurdle rate)
Step 7 — Payback & Break-even Tariff
Payback is the first year where cumulative cash flow ≥ 0 (simple, undiscounted).
Payback: first t where \( \sum_{\tau=0}^{t} CF_\tau \geq 0 \) Break-even tariff: tariff at which NPV = 0 (another bisection, 150 iters, range ₹0–30/kWh) Find tariff* such that: NPV(cashflows with rev=tariff*, dr) = 0 This is the minimum tariff needed for the project to exactly meet its cost of capital — like a "revenue-side LCOE" that also accounts for charging costs.
Step 8 — Monte Carlo Simulation (1,000 Runs)
The NPV distribution chart samples four uncertain inputs with uniform distributions around the base case. This shows the range of outcomes, not a single point estimate.
For each of 1,000 runs, independently sample: Capex ~ Uniform(cpx × 0.80, cpx × 1.20) [±20%] Cycles ~ Uniform(cyc × 0.85, cyc × 1.15) [±15%] Tariff ~ Uniform(rev × 0.75, rev × 1.25) [±25%] Chg cost ~ Uniform(chg × 0.85, chg × 1.15) [±15%] Each run: compute full cash flows → NPV Result: histogram of 1,000 NPVs Interpretation: Green bars = profitable outcomes (NPV ≥ 0) Red bars = loss-making outcomes Width of distribution = project risk
⚠ The simulation uses uniform distributions for simplicity. A more rigorous model would use log-normal for capex (right-skewed cost overruns) and triangular for tariff (policy-bounded). Correlation between cycles and tariff is also ignored — in reality, high-utilisation markets may command lower marginal tariffs.
Step 9 — Sensitivity Charts
Each sensitivity chart holds all other parameters fixed at their current slider values and sweeps one variable across its full range, computing NPV at each point.
Tariff sensitivity: sweep ₹3.0 to ₹15.0/kWh (20 steps of ₹0.6) NPV_i = NPV(params with rev = tariff_i) Capex sensitivity: sweep $80 to $270/kWh (20 steps of $10) NPV_i = NPV(params with cpx = capex_i) The zero-crossing on each chart = the break-even point for that variable (all else equal).
Model Assumptions & Limitations
1. Flat tariff: The model assumes a constant real tariff over the project life. In practice, PPAs may have escalation clauses (typically 0–3%/year). A 2% escalation materially improves NPV.

2. No battery degradation: Capacity and RTE are held constant over 15 years. Real LFP degrades ~2–3% per year in capacity, ~0.5% per year in RTE. This overstates late-year revenue.

3. No tax / depreciation: Accelerated depreciation (40%/yr for BESS in India) and MAT/CIT would reduce taxable income. The model is pre-tax; post-tax IRR would typically be 1.5–2.5% higher.

4. Constant O&M %: O&M as a fixed % of capex is a simplification. In practice, O&M grows in absolute terms as the system ages and warranty period ends (typically Year 5+).

5. Single revenue stream: The model assumes one blended storage tariff. Real projects often stack multiple revenue streams (ancillary + arbitrage + capacity payment). Multi-stream stacking can raise effective tariff by 15–30%.

6. Exchange rate risk: Capex is denominated in $ (battery import) but revenue in ₹. A 10% INR depreciation raises effective capex by ~₹9–11 Cr on a 100 MWh project. This model holds fx fixed.

Chart 2 — LCOE Forecast (₹/kWh): Wright's Law + BNEF Price Curve
The LCOE forecast uses Wright's Law (technology learning curve): each doubling of cumulative deployed capacity results in a fixed percentage cost reduction. For LFP batteries, the learning rate is approximately 18% (consistent with BNEF LCOS 2024 and historical lithium-ion cost data 2010–2024).
Wright's Law: Cost(Q₂) = Cost(Q₁) × (Q₂/Q₁)^log₂(1−LR) where LR = learning rate = 0.18 Each doubling of cumulative GWh deployed → 18% cost reduction Worked example (2025 → 2030): 2025 global cumulative: ~700 GWh 2030 global cumulative forecast: ~2,500 GWh Doublings: log₂(2500/700) = log₂(3.57) = 1.84 doublings Cost reduction: (1−0.18)^1.84 = 0.82^1.84 = 0.685 → 31.5% cost reduction from 2025 to 2030 LCOE anchors: 2025 LCOE: ₹4.4/kWh (base, from calculator defaults) 2030 LCOE: ₹2.5/kWh (base) = 4.4 × 0.685 → consistent Grid parity (~₹3/kWh) expected ~2027–2028 Scenario LCOE arrays (₹/kWh, 2024–2030): Bear: [5.2, 4.6, 4.1, 3.7, 3.4, 3.1, 2.9] (slower scale = slower learning) Base: [5.2, 4.4, 3.9, 3.4, 3.0, 2.7, 2.5] Bull: [5.2, 4.2, 3.6, 3.1, 2.7, 2.4, 2.1] (faster scale + domestic PLI)
The difference between scenarios reflects: (a) faster scale → more learning, and (b) in the Bull case, domestic PLI cell manufacturing removes the ~$25–30/kWh import cost premium India currently pays vs Chinese ex-factory prices.

Scenario Construction Logic
Each scenario is internally consistent across all four charts — the same growth rate drives both capacity and LCOE (via Wright's Law), and market revenue is derived from it.
BEAR scenario — what breaks: • Policy: VGF Phase 2 delayed 18+ months; CERC tariff order challenged • Demand: DISCOMs defer tenders due to state fiscal stress • Supply: PLI targets missed; domestic cells <10% of market by 2030 • Result: ~30 GWh — only mandatory co-location projects proceed • Probability: 20–25% (IEEFA base risk assessment) BASE scenario — what is priced in: • Policy: Steady execution of NESM; VGF phases on schedule • Cost: BNEF 18% learning rate; landed cost falls to $80/kWh by 2030 • Demand: SECI tenders converted at 60% LoA-to-commission rate • Result: ~51.5 GWh — consistent with NEP 2023 power target • Probability: 50–55% BULL scenario — what accelerates: • Policy: Mandatory co-location for all solar >200 MW from FY26 • Domestic PLI: Cells ramp to 15 GWh/yr domestic by 2028 → cost premium eliminated • C&I: Behind-the-meter market takes off as tariffs cross ₹3/kWh • Result: ~70 GWh — exceeds NEP power target by 70% • Probability: 20–25%
Forecast Limitations & What This Model Cannot Capture
1. Technology substitution: Solid-state batteries, sodium-ion, or vanadium flow may disrupt LFP's dominance post-2027. The model assumes LFP dominance throughout.

2. Grid constraint: Physical grid upgrade timelines (transmission capacity, STUs, load dispatch software) are not modelled. Grid bottlenecks could delay commissioning even with LoAs in hand.

3. China export policy: ~80% of BESS cells come from China. Any export restriction, tariff escalation, or geopolitical shock would spike capex and shift the Bear scenario probability upward.

4. DISCOM creditworthiness: India's DISCOMs carry ~₹6.5 lakh Cr in accumulated losses (UDAY II data). Payment security mechanism (PSM/ESCROW) is available but project-by-project. This is not reflected in tariff or revenue assumptions.

5. Second-life BESS: By 2028–2030, retired EV batteries (from Ola, Tata, etc.) could enter the stationary storage market at $20–30/kWh, potentially disrupting new greenfield economics. Not modelled.

6. Inflation & FX: All forecasts are in nominal ₹ but treat FX and inflation as static. A sustained 5%+ INR depreciation would increase $/kWh-denominated capex by ~5% annually.
Regression Methodology (Summary)
Capacity: Exponential fit y=ae^(bt) on 2019–2025 data (R²=0.97). Bear/Bull ±35% on growth rate b, anchored to NEP 2023 target.

LCOE: Wright's Law — 18% cost reduction per doubling of global cumulative capacity. Anchored to BNEF 2025 benchmark ($130/kWh pack → ₹4.4/kWh LCOE).

Revenue: Annual additions × system cost + operational fleet × utilisation × blended tariff (TAM basis, ICRA-BNEF methodology).

Signal & Energy Flow

⑤ Transformer Steps up 614V DC-side to 33/132/400kV. Protection relay handles islanding, ROCOF, over-current faults.
⑥ SCADA/EMS Receives dispatch from SLDC via IEC 61850. Optimises between FCR, SFR, peak-shave, and arbitrage.

Duck Curve — RE Overgeneration & BESS Role
Rajasthan grid · Summer day
Understanding the Duck Curve

Named for its resemblance to a duck, the curve shows the gap between total electricity demand and renewable energy production throughout the day.

Belly (10am–3pm): Solar floods the grid → prices crash, possible curtailment. Net load dips dangerously low.

Neck (4–7pm): Solar drops suddenly → massive ramp-up needed in <3 hours. Grid stress point.

BESS Solution: Absorbs excess midday solar (charging), then discharges during the evening ramp — flattening the curve.

India ramp challenge: ~15 GW in 3 hours by 2030 (Rajasthan alone). Needs 8–10 GWh BESS statewide.
